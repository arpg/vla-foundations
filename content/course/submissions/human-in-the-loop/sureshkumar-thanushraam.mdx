# Safe Autonomy in a Domestic Humanoid Robot: Handling Hazardous Tools Under Latency and Uncertainty

## Scenerio 

Humanoid robots operating in homes must function in unstructured, human-centered environments such as kitchens,
where people are nearby and object risks depend on context.

Consider a robot preparing food while handling a knife.
High-level planning may run on a separate model or remote system,
while low-level controllers execute physical motion.
If latency or desynchronization occurs between these layers, the robot’s internal state may diverge from the real world.

For instance, the planner may assume the knife has been placed down and proceed to the next task, while the robot is still holding it.
A motion that would normally be safe such as reaching for a bowl can therefore become hazardous.

This scenario highlights three key challenges for safe autonomy: **failing safely during system inconsistencies,
defining autonomy boundaries for hazardous tools, and maintaining trust when humans intervene under latency or uncertainty.**

---
## Failing Safely

Beyond software crashes or sensor faults, humanoid robots can experience state desynchronization,
where the planner believes an action is finished while the physical system has not confirmed it.

In the kitchen example, the planner may believe the knife has been placed down and allow the robot to reach for another object.
In reality, the robot may still be gripping the knife, creating a risk of accidental contact with nearby humans or objects.

To address this, the robot should **fail toward reduced capability rather than continuing the task**.
When uncertainty about hazardous tool possession is detected, the system should enter a **degraded safety mode**.

A safe response could include:

1. **Verifying the state of hazardous objects** before initiating new actions
    
2. **Adopting a guarded posture**, keeping the blade oriented away from humans
    
3. **Blocking new planner actions** until the knife is safely released
    
4. **Alerting a supervising human** if the uncertainty cannot be resolved
    
5. **Maintaining safe distance**, resolving the situation through teleoperation or supervision

These measures avoid two dangerous extremes: blindly continuing motion or freezing unpredictably, by placing the robot in a **minimal-risk condition**
until the system state is verified.

---
## Defining the Autonomy Boundary

Another key challenge in domestic robotics is determining which decisions can be handled autonomously and which must be deferred to human oversight.
In kitchen environments, tasks vary widely in risk, so the autonomy boundary should depend on three factors:

- **Risk level of the object involved**
    
- **Reversibility of the action**
    
- **Confidence in the robot’s perception and execution state**

**Actions Suitable for Autonomous Execution**

The robot can safely perform routine tasks when risk and uncertainty are low.
These include:
- reaching for utensils or ingredients
    
- stirring or mixing food
    
- transporting lightweight objects
    
- placing items on surfaces
    
- cleaning or organizing items

These actions are generally reversible and pose minimal harm if executed incorrectly.

**Constrained Autonomy for Hazardous Tools**

Handling a knife introduces higher risk and requires stricter constraints. When manipulating hazardous objects, the robot should:

- restrict cutting motions to a designated workspace such as a cutting board
    
- continuously track the knife’s position and orientation
    
- enforce human proximity safety zones
    
- disable reaching for unrelated objects while holding the knife
    
- verify that the knife has been placed down before starting a new task

Holding a hazardous tool should therefore place the robot in a **restricted operational mode**, where its range of allowed behaviors is significantly reduced.

**Handling Harmful or Illegal Human Commands**

Robots must also handle situations where humans issue **unsafe or harmful commands**.
For example, a person might instruct the robot to pick up a knife and harm another individual.
Even though the command comes from a human, the robot must refuse to execute it.

Domestic humanoid robots should therefore include **policy-level safety constraints** that override direct user instructions when those actions could cause harm.

In such cases, the robot should:

- refuse commands that involve harming humans
    
- prioritize safety over obedience
    
- escalate the situation to a supervisory system if needed
    
- enter a safe standby state

These safeguards ensure that **human-in-the-loop supervision does not become human-directed harm**, maintaining fundamental safety rules regardless of user intent.

**Situations Requiring Human Intervention**

Some states should always trigger a pause and require human supervision. 
These include:

* uncertainty about whether the robot is still holding the knife

* loss of confidence in blade pose estimation

* sensor or perception disagreement about object state

* unexpected human entry into the active workspace

* planner–execution desynchronization

* prolonged communication latency during hazardous manipulation

In these cases, the robot should stop advancing the task until a human confirms the appropriate next step.

---

## Trust and Latency in Human Oversight

Human oversight may be delayed or intermittent, especially when high-level reasoning runs on remote systems or cloud infrastructure.
When communication resumes after a delay, the operator must quickly understand the robot’s state to decide how to proceed.

Instead of presenting raw logs or video feeds, the robot should provide a **structured summary of its uncertainty**.

This summary should include:

**State freshness**  
Perception inputs and system estimates should include timestamps so the operator knows how recent the information is.

**Confidence indicators**  
The robot should report whether subsystems agree about the current state.
For example, perception may detect the knife while grasp sensors indicate it was released.

**Action constraints**  
The interface should clearly indicate which actions are currently allowed or blocked, such as:

- “Knife possession uncertain”
    
- “New motion commands inhibited”
    
- “Safe placement routine available”
Presenting information in this structured way allows operators to quickly understand the situation without reconstructing the robot’s internal reasoning.

---

## Managing Cognitive Load During Reconnection

  When communication is restored after a delay, the operator should not be overwhelmed with a backlog of events. 
  Instead, the system should provide a concise summary of what occurred during the disconnection.

An effective reconnection interface might include:

**Event summary**
* cutting task started

* planner issued placement command

* placement confirmation not received

* planner initiated reach action

* state mismatch detected

* robot entered guarded safety mode

**Current robot state**
* object possession: uncertain

* blade orientation: tracked

* arm position: safe posture

* human proximity: clear

**Pending decisions**

* confirm knife placement or execute safe placement routine

* resume cooking task or switch to standby mode

This structured summary allows the operator to quickly understand the situation and make safe decisions when reconnecting to the system.

---
Safe domestic robots must be designed to detect uncertainty, restrict autonomy when risks increase,
and clearly communicate their state to human supervisors.
These principles ensure that when failures occur, the system prioritizes human safety over task completion.
