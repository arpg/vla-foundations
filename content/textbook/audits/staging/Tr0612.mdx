# Introduction

**What is Mixture-of-Experts?**

Mixture of Expert(MoE) is a approach that divides an AI model into sub-networks or experts, each specializing in a subset of the input data, to jointly perform a task.
Instead of activating every parameter for every input, MoE dynamically selects a small number of experts through a learned routing or gating mechanism.
This enables models to increase representational capacity without incurring proportional increases in computational cost.

At a high level, an MoE architecture consists of:

- **Expert networks** – typically feed-forward networks (FFNs) or modules that specialize in different subspaces of the input distribution.
- **Routing / gating function** – a learned mechanism that determines which experts are activated for a given input.
- **Sparse execution** – only a subset (e.g., top-k experts per token) is executed per input, reducing active computation.

The core promise of MoE is simple but powerful: **capacity can scale faster than compute.**

## Scope of This Audit

This audit examines the use of Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) in Vision-Language-Action (VLA) systems, with a focus on ChatVLA-2 and ForceVLA.
The goal is to assess whether MoE improves the reasoning–action interface rather than adding architectural complexity.
I also discuss whether the added design complexity is justified by the observed gains.

## Historical Context: MoE

| Phase     | Language                                | Vision                           |
| --------- | --------------------------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| 1990s     | Ensemble-style MoE                      | —                                |
| 2017      | Sparsely-Gated MoE enables scaling      | —                                |
| 2020–2021 | Switch, scalable LLM MoE                | V-MoE extends to vision          |
| 2022–2024 | Routing refinements, deployment systems | Multi-task & multimodal MoE      |
| Today     | Core LLM infrastructure                 | Efficient large-scale perception |

Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) originated as an ensemble-based neural network technique introduced by Jacobs et al.(1991) [1], 
where a gating network dynamically selects among multiple expert subnetworks.
Its modern resurgence began with the sparsely gated MoE layer proposed by Noam Shazeer et al.(2017) [2], which enabled conditional computation 
in deep transformers by activating only a small subset of experts per token.
This broke the tight coupling between parameter count and computation, allowing language models to scale to billions or trillions of parameters efficiently.
Subsequent works such as Switch Transformers [3] and systems like DeepSpeed-MoE [5] improved training stability, routing simplicity, and deployment efficiency, 
establishing MoE as a core scaling strategy in large language models.
The paradigm later extended to vision through Vision Mixture-of-Experts (V-MoE) [4], which integrated sparse expert routing into the Vision Transformer architecture, 
demonstrating that conditional computation can scale vision models to billions of parameters while preserving accuracy and reducing compute costs.
Collectively, these developments show MoE evolving from an ensemble method into a general architectural principle for scalable, efficient, and specialized modeling across language and vision.

# Paper Audit: ChatVLA-2

ChatVLA-2 [9] positions Mixture-of-Experts not as a scaling mechanism, but as an **interface control mechanism** between multimodal reasoning and robotic action execution.

The central claim is that tightly coupling reasoning and control degrades either:

- reasoning generalization (if fine-tuned too aggressively), or
- control stability (if reasoning dominates action layers).

This paper proposes that this boundary should be explicitly managed and introduces Dynamic MoE as the mechanism to do so.

It arises one central question:

> Does their MoE architecture meaningfully stabilize the reasoning–action interface,or just adding a smarter-looking middle layer that hides it?

**Where is MoE applied ?**

ChatVLA-2 integrates a **Dynamic Mixture-of-Experts layer** within the VLA architecture, selectively activating experts based on multimodal input representations.

Eight experts are instantiated, and two are selected per input during inference.

However, the important design decision is not the number of experts, it is **where intervention occurs**.

Rather than restructuring the pretrained VLM backbone, ChatVLA-2 preserves it and injects MoE primarily within the action pathway.
This limits disturbance to pretrained reasoning weights.

This reflects an assumption:

> The pretrained VLM already encodes strong world knowledge, and instability arises when action learning corrupts that representation.

---

**Why Dynamic Routing ?**

Static expert assignment risks:

- over-specialization
- expert collapse
- suppression of pretrained knowledge

Dynamic routing allows input-dependent expert activation, preventing consistent bias toward a fixed expert subset.

However, this introduces a new dependency:

- The gating network becomes a bottleneck.
- Routing errors may propagate directly into action instability.

An important question to investigate:

> What signal does the router use to separate reasoning complexity from control complexity?

If routing is dominated by visual similarity, the system may still overfit robot-specific domains.

---

**Aligning Reasoning with Action**

A defining contribution of ChatVLA-2 is the claim that actions must not merely follow instructions, but follow **generated reasoning traces**.

Unlike FiLM-based approaches (e.g., DiffusionVLA, DexVLA), which inject reasoning tokens uniformly, ChatVLA-2:

- Projects reasoning tokens via MLP
- Combines them with timestep embeddings
- Conditions scale/shift parameters
- Injects them only in later layers

This selective conditioning suggests:

- Early layers preserve low-level motor stability
- Later layers adapt to reasoning

The architectural philosophy here is :

> Modify only layers that minimally disturb control primitives.

This raises a couple of question:

- Is reasoning alignment achieved because of MoE?
- Or because of late-layer conditioning?
- Would the model perform similarly without MoE but with staged freezing?

---

**Interface Design and Assumptions**

ChatVLA-2 implicitly defines a three-part boundary:

1. Pretrained reasoning backbone
2. Dynamic expert selection layer
3. Action-conditioned decoding

MoE operates as a mediator.

However, several interface assumptions remain implicit:

- Does expert routing vary across time steps?
- Is routing consistent within a trajectory?
- Is expert selection correlated with reasoning tokens or visual features?

In embodied systems, even slight routing instability can produce compounding control errors.

This is where VLA differs from LLMs:
Routing errors affect physics.

## Training

ChatVLA-2 is trained through co-training on both image–text data and robot trajectories to support open-world reasoning and embodied control.

![Training Strategy](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/arpg/vla-foundations/audit/Tr0612-mixtureofexpert/content/textbook/audits/staging/Training_Strategy_ChatVLA2.png)

### Training Data

**Image–Text Data**
The model leverages a mixture of established benchmarks and task-aligned datasets:

- ~32k samples from **COCO**
- ~20k from **TextVQA**
- ~54k from **GQA**
- ~2k robotics-related samples from **RoboPoint** (simulated)
- ~5k real-world robotics samples (tabletop and broader scenes)

Robotics-related image–text pairs follow the same reasoning template used in the toy placement task.
Text augmentation is applied to increase query diversity.
To avoid biasing the model toward specific skills (e.g., OCR or math), numeric-heavy TextVQA samples are filtered out.
Each example is limited to 5 dialogue turns, and images are resized to 320×240.

**Robot Data**
includes:

- 600 trajectories from a math-matching task
- 300 trajectories from a toy placement task

All trajectories are annotated with structured reasoning phrases.
These annotations are initialized with fixed templates and then diversified using GPT-4o to prevent overfitting to rigid reasoning patterns.

**Training Setup**

- Image–text to robot data ratio: 1:3
- Total training steps: 50k
- Initial learning rate: 2e-5
- 3k-step warm-up
- Cosine decay to 2e-6

Overall, the training strategy aims to preserve pretrained multimodal knowledge while grounding it in robotic control, without explicitly biasing the model toward specific reasoning skills.

### Training Strategy & Implementation

The model adopts a two-stage training strategy aligned with its modular design.

1. In Stage 1, the model is jointly trained on image–text and robotic data to develop both general reasoning and embodied understanding.
2. In Stage 2, the Vision-Language Model (VLM) backbone is frozen and only the action experts are updated.
This design is motivated by the concern that jointly optimizing reasoning and control objectives may cause gradient interference in shared representations.

While freezing the VLM helps preserve pretrained reasoning capabilities, it may also retain biases from large-scale image–text data and limit adaptation to robot-specific dynamics.

Training is conducted in mixed precision (FP16) using AdamW.
Stage 1 runs for 15k steps with a learning rate of 2e-5.
Stage 2 runs for 50k steps, with a 3k-step warm-up and cosine decay from 2e-5 to 2e-6.
The total reported training cost is approximately **340 GPU hours**.

## Critical Synthesis & Sign off

### Load Bearing Assumptions

**Does freezing the VLM preserve or limit reasoning?**
Ablations show both training stages are necessary.
Removing Stage 2 (action-only training with a frozen VLM) significantly reduces robotic performance, indicating that reasoning features must be explicitly grounded into action execution.
Removing Stage 1 eliminates open-world reasoning entirely, leading to near-zero OCR and math performance.
While this confirms the importance of both stages, it remains unclear whether freezing the VLM merely protects pretrained reasoning or restricts its adaptation to embodied constraints.

**Is MoE necessary in VLA?**
Comparisons between dense and MoE variants show that removing MoE substantially reduces success on math-matching tasks, and scaling a dense model to 7B does not recover performance.
This suggests the advantage is not simply model size.
The authors argue that dense models suffer from interference between action and reasoning representations, whereas MoE mitigates this via expert specialization.
Notably, dense models fail entirely on unseen equations, indicating that without MoE, robotic fine-tuning may degrade reasoning robustness under distribution shift.

### Potential Failure Modes

1. **Routing Instability**

   - Does the model switch between experts too frequently over time?
   - Could these rapid changes lead to unstable or jerky control?

2. **Expert Underuse**

   - Are some experts rarely used during training or inference?
   - Does the routing system actually keep experts diverse and active?

3. **Reasoning Errors Affecting Actions**

   - If the reasoning output is wrong, will the system still follow it blindly when generating actions?
   - Is there any mechanism to detect or correct such errors?

4. **Compute Cost vs. Benefit**

   - Does the added complexity of Dynamic MoE justify the performance improvements?
   - The paper shows accuracy gains but does not report compute costs such as FLOPs or latency, making it difficult to assess efficiency.

### Reproducibility

The paper clearly describes the architecture, staged training strategy, and ablation settings, and the public release of code substantially supports research reproducibility.
Given the reported hardware requirements, replication appears feasible in well-equipped academic or industrial research environments.

### Sign-Off Criteria

I would not sign this system off for production deployment without additional validation on long-horizon tasks, stability of expert utilization, and clearly defined safety and recovery procedures.
Further robustness testing is necessary before considering real-world deployment.

# Paper Audit: ForceVLA 

The paper argues that existing VLA models struggle with contact-rich manipulation tasks that require precise force control, especially under visual occlusion or dynamic uncertainty.
To address this gap, the authors propose **ForceVLA** [10], an end-to-end framework that integrates external force sensing as a core modality within VLA systems.
The work is further supported by a newly collected multimodal dataset tailored for contact-rich manipulation.

**Contribution**

The central contribution is **FVLMoE**, a force-aware Mixture-of-Experts fusion module that dynamically integrates pretrained vision-language embeddings with real-time 6-axis force feedback during action decoding.
By introducing structured, adaptive force fusion, the model enables context-sensitive and phase-aware action generation, improving performance in tasks that depend on fine-grained physical interaction.

## Architecture Summary

![alt text](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/arpg/vla-foundations/audit/Tr0612-mixtureofexpert/content/textbook/audits/staging/ForceVLA_Architecture.png)

ForceVLA extends a Vision-Language-Action (VLA) model by introducing **FVLMoE**, a force-aware Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) fusion module designed for contact-rich manipulation.
The key idea is to treat force as a first-class modality and integrate it *after* visual–language encoding, enabling adaptive, phase-aware action generation.

### Overall Pipeline

1. **Vision–Language Encoding**
   - RGB images and task instructions are processed by a pretrained Vision-Language Model (VLM).
   - This produces contextual visual-language tokens
     $$E_{VL} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_{VL} \times D_{model}}$$
2. **Force Token Construction**
   - Raw 6-axis force-torque readings
     $$f_{raw} \in \mathbb{R}^6$$
     are projected via a linear layer:
     $$E_F = \phi_F(f_{raw}) \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{model}}$$
   - This produces a **dedicated force token** aligned to the VLM embedding dimension.
3. **Token Concatenation**
   - The force token is appended to the visual-language tokens:
     $E_{in} = [E_{VL}; E_F]$
   - Importantly, force is injected **after** the VLM encoder to preserve pretrained representations (validated via ablation studies).

### Architecture of FVLMoE: Force-Aware Mixture-of-Experts

FVLMoE performs multimodal fusion using a sparse Mixture-of-Experts architecture inspired by:

- *Scaling Vision with Sparse Mixture of Experts* [4]
- *GShard* [12]

#### Stage 1: Shared Encoder

The concatenated tokens pass through:

- Multi-head self-attention
- Feed-forward network (FFN)

This enables holistic interaction across visual, language, and force tokens.

#### Stage 2: Sparse MoE Fusion

The core fusion mechanism consists of:

- **E = 4 expert networks**
  
  - Each expert is an independent MLP
  - Experts implicitly specialize in different modality interactions or task phases
- **Gating Network (Router)**
  
  - Takes each token as input
  - Produces routing logits
  - Selects the top-1 expert (k=1) per token
  - Computes routing weights

Formally:

$$
y(x) = \sum_{i \in \text{TopK}(G(x))} g_i(x) E_i(x)
$$

where:

- $G(x)$ = router
- $E_i$ = expert networks
- $g_i(x)$ = routing weights

Only one expert processes each token (sparse routing), improving efficiency and encouraging specialization.

---

**What Experts Exist?**

There are **four MLP experts**, each capable of learning distinct behaviors such as:

- Visual-dominant reasoning
- Language-conditioned planning
- Force-reactive corrections
- Phase-specific control (e.g., alignment vs insertion vs contact stabilization)

Router analysis shows:

- Some experts activate consistently across tasks (general-purpose expert).
- Others specialize in specific task phases (e.g., insertion contact phase).

---

**How Routing Is Conditioned**

Routing decisions depend on:

1. **High-level semantic context**
   - Task instructions
   - Visual scene embeddings
2. **Low-level interaction feedback**
   - Real-time force signals
   - Task progress state

Because force is included as a token in the shared sequence, routing is conditioned jointly on:

- Vision
- Language
- Force

This allows dynamic expert selection across different manipulation phases.

---

**Action Generation (Flow-Based Head)**

The fused multimodal features are used to guide a **flow-matching action decoder**:

1. The final $H_{action}$ fused tokens are extracted.
2. These are element-wise added to:
   - Proprioceptive state embeddings
   - Noisy action trajectory embeddings during denoising.
3. The flow model iteratively refines the action trajectory.

This ensures:

- Force-aware corrections
- Phase-aligned motion generation
- Stable contact control

---

**Key Design Insights**

- **Late force fusion** preserves pretrained VLM representations.
- **Sparse MoE routing** enables modality- and phase-aware specialization.
- **Dynamic gating** allows adaptation to contact dynamics in real time.
- Effective fusion (not just force input) is critical for performance gains.

## Critical Synthesis & Sign-Off

### Load-Bearing Assumptions

**Is estimated force sufficient for stable control?**
ForceVLA relies on estimated external wrench values, assuming that these signals are accurate enough for precise manipulation.
This presumes that estimation noise, drift, or calibration error does not meaningfully degrade performance, and that learned policies can compensate for sensing inaccuracies.
However, in high-precision tasks such as tight insertions, deformable object manipulation, or complex assemblies, small force estimation errors may significantly impact stability and success.

**Does performance generalize across different sensing hardware?**
The experiments use high quality force–torque sensors, which implicitly assumes that the learned policy is not strongly dependent on sensor quality.
This suggests that low cost or retrofitted sensing setups might still provide signals that are reliable enough for control.
However, force-based control is often sensitive to latency, noise, and calibration errors.
As a result, this assumption may not hold when the system is deployed across different hardware setups with varying sensor fidelity.

**Is architecture the primary bottleneck to accessibility?**
The authors suggest that adapting ForceVLA to more affordable platforms could democratize force-aware manipulation.
This assumes that architectural innovation, rather than sensing quality, is the dominant limitation.
However, in contact-rich manipulation, hardware constraints may fundamentally limit achievable performance, regardless of model sophistication.

### Potential Failure Modes

1. **Sensitivity to Force Estimation**

   - Does the system perform worse if the force estimates are noisy or inaccurate?
   - Can the policy handle sensor drift or delays in force measurements?

2. **Stability Across Different Hardware**

   - Does the model need retraining or recalibration when used with different sensors or hardware?
   - Could small calibration differences cause unstable behavior?

3. **Overfitting to Specific Contacts**

   - Can the model generalize to new materials or objects with different stiffness or compliance?

# Additional Analysis

## MoE leads to better reasoning or memorization

Across the audited papers, I observed substantial variation in the number of experts used in MoE-based architectures.
Although ChatVLA reports performance differences for different number of experts, this raises a question: 
does increasing the number of experts genuinely improve reasoning, especially when computational overhead is not a constraint?

Mixture of Parrots paper [13] directly address this issue.
Their work shows that MoE models exhibit fundamentally different scaling dynamics for memorization and reasoning tasks, leading to two important conclusions.

First, reasoning performance depends on hidden dimension (width), not merely the number of experts.
In their study of graph reasoning problems such as connectivity and shortest path, they prove that a sparse MoE Transformer cannot solve certain tasks unless its hidden dimension exceeds a critical threshold.
*Simply increasing the number of experts does not overcome insufficient width*.
By contrast, a dense Transformer with slightly greater width can successfully solve these same tasks.
This suggests that reasoning capacity is tied to active representational bandwidth rather than total distributed parameters.

Second, memorization improves as the number of experts increases.
For tasks that require remembering input–label pairs, MoE models can store large amounts of information while only activating a small portion of the model during computation.
In these cases, memorization depends more on the **total number of parameters** in the model rather than the **number of parameters actively used during inference**.
As a result, adding more experts increases the model’s storage capacity, even if it does not improve its reasoning ability.

Taken together, these findings imply that scaling experts in MoE architectures may primarily strengthen memorization rather than reasoning, 
a distinction that I feel particularly relevant when evaluating MoE-based VLA systems.
